Trail Difficulty Clustering

Showcasing clustering model behind the "trails-to-health" recommendation tool.

Clustering for mixed type data

November 20, 2023 by Raj De

The Challenge of Quantifying Trail Difficulty

Trail difficulty assessment is a crucial aspect of any trail recommendation system. However, determining the level of difficulty for a trail is not a straightforward task. Numerous factors come into play, such as trail length, elevation gain, angle of descent, and type of trail (whether it's a remote path or a well-marked route). One significant question arises: how do we accurately weigh each of these factors to arrive at a reliable difficulty rating?

The Initial Approach: Manual Weighting

Initially, I attempted to assign manual weight factors to each attribute based on my discretion. This method, while straightforward, was highly subjective and lacked a factual, data-driven foundation. A more objective and robust approach was necessary to eliminate personal bias and ensure consistency in the difficulty ratings.

Embracing Unsupervised Learning: Clustering Algorithms

To achieve a more data-driven approach, I turned to unsupervised learning techniques, specifically clustering algorithms. These algorithms are designed to group data points into clusters based on their similarities, without any human intervention. Several clustering techniques were considered, including K-means clustering, Hierarchical clustering (both Agglomerative and Divisive approaches), DBSCAN, and its extension HDBSCAN.

Selecting the Right Algorithm for Mixed Data Types

Clustering algorithms are pivotal in data science for grouping data points with similar characteristics. Common clustering techniques include K-means for partitioning, hierarchical methods such as Agglomerative clustering, and density-based approaches like HDBSCAN.

Adapting Categorical Data for Agglomerative Clustering

Agglomerative clustering is traditionally suited for numerical data. To incorporate categorical data, such as the type of trail, I used one-hot encoding, which transforms categorical variables into a binary matrix—effectively treating them as numerical data. This approach allowed for the seamless integration of mixed data types into the clustering analysis.

The Limitation of Agglomerative Clustering with Mixed Data

Despite the initial integration, Agglomerative clustering did not effectively disaggregate the categorical data's influence on the results. To address this, I turned to K-Prototypes—a robust algorithm that combines K-means and K-modes, ideal for clustering mixed data types. K-modes clustering handles categorical variables by measuring the modes instead of means, allowing for the categorization of non-numeric data points based on the frequency of categories.

Optimizing Cluster Count with the Elbow Method

Finding the sweet spot for the number of clusters is key to getting our groupings just right. So, I plotted an elbow plot to see where the benefits of adding more clusters start to taper off. It turns out that at cluster number four, we hit that point – the elbow. That's where the drop in within-cluster variation isn't as steep anymore. Adding more clusters beyond this point doesn't really do us much good, as the gains are pretty minimal. So, we decided to go with four clusters since that's where our plot showed the best balance.

Implementing K-Prototypes for Refined Clustering

By implementing K-Prototypes, I was able to effectively cluster the data, achieving clear disaggregation based on the categorical variables. The resulting clusters were more representative of the underlying patterns in the data, providing a nuanced understanding of the dataset.

Conclusion: A Data-Driven Approach to Trail Difficulty

Switching from the old-school method of just eyeballing weights to figure out trail difficulty, we moved to a more data-driven approach. We started off with Agglomerative clustering and threw in some one-hot encoding to mix numerical and categorical data. But the real change was when we brought in K-Prototypes. This worked really well for our mixed bag of data, cutting through the noise and giving us neat, bias-free clusters.

To make sure we got the number of clusters right, we didn't just guess – we used an elbow plot. Now, new trail seekers can hit up the Trails to Health app and not feel overwhelmed by choices.
